Arcade

Overview:

Modern games are immersive, thilling, and fun to play with friends. They feature state-of-the-art graphics and advanced physics algorithms that make the game-world look real. In some people’s opinion however, no modern game could ever best classic arcade games.
While this nostalgic community exists and accounts for a surprisingly large percent of the gaming community, there is no game system that is for sale that will play arcade games. One could purchase a 40 year old arcade machine, but they are expensive (about $5,000) and only play one game. So, in order to play these classic games, members of the community have come up with a solution — it's called RetroPie. 

What is RetroPie:

RetroPie is a computer software that is designed specifically to run arcade games. It is built upon the Raspbian operating system, used on Raspberry Pi computers. These credit-card sized computers are commonly used in many hobby projects, ranging from robotics to smart-home appliances. When installed on the Raspberry Pi, RetroPie will be able to emulate over 50 different game systems.

Building Custom Controllers:

RetroPie is compatible with XBox and PlayStation controllers. Using these controllers, RetroPie will support up to four players in a single game. For a simple setup, a player could play with these controllers and have a great experience. However, using modern controllers does not feel authentic. An arcade needs the joystick, and super clicky buttons. To obtain this authenticity, custom controllers and the arcade cabinet needs to be built.
 

Luckly, there is a U.K. based company called Ultimarc that sells arcade controller parts. They sell buttons, joysticks, coin buckets, and much more. I kept it simple for this project, and bought the essentials: 2 eight-way joysticks and 10 buttons. 

This will provide maximum compatibility with games, without going over budget.
These buttons and joysticks all needed to be wired to a pcb control board. Ultimarc makes a board that takes electrical inputs and converts them to keyboard button clicks. This will interface perfectly with the RetroPie.  

The Cabinet:

This is where it is easy to break the bank. The cabinet could be made of machined aluminum and feature custom vinyl side graphics, speakers, and back lit face graphics. For this build, I wanted to keep it simple. I bought a couple sheets of finished plywood, and a can of black paint from the hardware store. I used a reciprocating saw to make all the cuts, and used wood screws to hold it all together. The cuts could have been a bit cleaner with better tools, but I made it work with what I had. The custom controller I built is permanently housed in the cabinet.

The result:
After a few weeks of research, and a couple of weeks of construction, I completed my very own personal arcade. This machine is two player, and emulates over 60 classic titles.
